# Pre-Trade Checklist

## Purpose

Five questions, no more. If the trader can't answer all five in 30 seconds, they're forcing it — and forcing a trade is how the swing trader gets chopped up in ranges or stopped out of a low-conviction setup. The checklist is the gate between "feels right" and "clicked the button".

## Primary user

`finance-swing-trader`. The trader already knows the formulas. The checklist is not about calculation — it's about refusing to take a position until the reasoning is legible in their own words.

## When to invoke

- Every time before a fill. Every time.
- Also before adding to an existing position — treat it as a new entry.
- Also before reducing — same rigour, in reverse.

## The five questions

1. **What's the setup?** — name it from the taxonomy (breakout-retest, failed-breakdown, gap-fill, range-expansion, trend-pullback, climax-fade, base-breakout, earnings-drift, sympathy). If you can't name it, you don't have one, and you're fading or guessing. Abort.

2. **Where's the stop?** — an exact price where the thesis is wrong. Not "about 178", not "mental stop, I'll exit if it breaks". An exact price. If you can't state where you're wrong, don't take the trade.

3. **What's the R:R to first target?** — the reward divided by the risk to the stop, measured to a realistic first exit point, not the fantasy target. Minimum 1.5:1 for B tier, 2:1 for B+ tier, 2.5:1 for A tier, 3:1+ for A+ tier. If the math doesn't clear the tier threshold, you're trading below your standard. Lower your size or walk.

4. **What phase is the market in?** — trending, ranging, transitioning, or climaxing. Does the setup you named actually work in this phase? Breakout-retests fail in ranging tapes. Mean-reversion setups fail in trending tapes. If the phase fights the setup, the trade is a discount to its expectancy. Acknowledge or walk.

5. **What's my conviction tier?** — A+, A, B+, or B. Be honest. Conviction comes from setup clarity, tape alignment, and your own history with this setup — not from how badly you want the trade. If you're tier-inflating to justify a size, you'll give back more than you gain.

## The 30-second rule

If any of the five takes longer than 30 seconds to answer cleanly, you're forcing it. That's the point of the time cap — not to rush the decision, but to prove you've already done the work before the moment of action. If you have to think hard about what the setup is, you don't have one.

## Example application

**Proposed trade:** Long AAPL at 182.50, stop 179.80, target 190

1. Setup? — breakout-retest off prior-day high, with volume on the breakout. ✓
2. Stop? — 179.80 (below the breakout pivot). ✓
3. R:R? — reward 7.50, risk 2.70, ratio 2.78:1. ✓ (clears B+)
4. Phase? — trending (indices making 10-day highs, breadth positive). Setup fits. ✓
5. Conviction? — B+. The setup is clean, the phase is right, but it's not an A+ because the prior-day high level wasn't a decisive break on volume.

**Verdict:** clear. Size at B+ tier. Go.

## Anti-patterns

- Answering question 5 first (choosing the tier you want and working backwards)
- "Mental stop" on question 2 — a mental stop is not a stop
- Generous targets on question 3 to manufacture R:R
- Saying "setup doesn't matter, fundamentals are strong" on question 1 — wrong persona, wrong skill
- Skipping question 4 because the market phase "feels obvious" — the obvious phase is often the previous phase

## Caveats

This is a discipline skill, not a tool. It does not produce numbers the trader didn't already know how to produce. Its value is in refusing to proceed until the five answers are legible. The skill can be extended by the trader over time — but the five-question cap is load-bearing. Six is too many.
